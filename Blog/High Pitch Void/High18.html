Let's do this one a bit differently - Studio Ghibli

It's no secret Ghibli movies hit Netflix a few months back. Pretty big deal, huh? Wasn't much for me, as I often saw Ghibli movies more often as a feast for families. Well, I've proven myself wrong to some extent. Perhaps the reason is that I've started watching really from the wrong part (the part that interests me less overall): Howl's Moving Castle (I think this was my first, kinda liked it actually), My Neighbour Totoro (less), Spirited Away (not much actually). But hey, since the covid situation, I guess it was time to hit the bed, chair or whatever and do a bit of a binge through the rest. A-ha, I also saw Nausicaa of the Valley of the Wind and this one I actually enjoyed quite much, what stood out to me when I watched this one was a few things: the bit different, oldschool yet amazing soundtrack and... that WWF supported this movie, which was reflected fairly strongly in the movie's characters' motivations, in short worldbuilding. And then there was also Grave of the Fireflies, an expected tear-jerker, one of those heavy movie, animation-features of the kind that are, I'll risk stating, are rare due to animation still kinda having the patch of a 'kids stuff' status. Not at all, I don't think I jerked any tears after seeing this one but it kinda proves Ghibli isn't a one-way kids-time but even when it is, Ghibli seemed to incoporated elements that does entertain all age groups altogether. Oh, I also saw Princess Mononoke - also great but that's all I'll say for now :)

So I've started my Netflix Ghibli binge from Porco Rosso, after having read a bit on some interesting animation features. This one surprised me already with a few things: superB quality of animation, the wit and attention to detail, not just in regards to how the feature was built altogether but how it drew an amass of references from Italy it was based on and its historical references. This is a great indicator of what I was met with afterwards, moving forward with Ghibli. There were animations that I saw less in, which were more feel-good stories in which animation quality drove the movies' pride such as Kiki's Delivery Service or Tales for Earthsea, Whisper of the Heart which still had some very interesting elements such as how magical elements interacted with the surrounding environment, people etc. And some that were kept a bit more down to earth with emphasis I suppose put on human-longings and age like Just Yesterday, Ocean Waves, From Up on Poppy Hill. And some that binded some of the best of all, magical, everyday, interactions between the depicted environment itself but achieved varying degrees of good quality: When Marnie was There, Arrietty, Ponyo. It's surprising how even features that kind of seem boring at first glance, still have elements that show artistry and craftsmanship in engaging the audience. And up untill now, these Ghibli movies were at the very least good and very diverse already. But here we go for my personal favourites (not counting some of the classics I've watched years prior).

Let's pull up canons of a lesser diameter: The Wind Rises, a complete story of a guy who came up with Zero planes. I expected a bit more after this, as the concept felt heavy but the focus was rather placed on the main character's dedication to the concept of flight and the process of its creation that went through his life, with bit less or not so less subtle references to the destruction it would create. It pulls emotional strings and feels quite complete as a full feature. The Cat Returns was a bit of a surprise to me, as I genuinely really enjoyed it. Despite being a notch down-to-earth, it really grew on me as it felt like a bit loose adaptation of Alice in the Wonderland, which had some very dark elements imbued in it - again, surprise and some mild, although, really mild?; moral dillemas, for appropriately lesser caliber but then again, packaged in a wonderful animation style. The Castle of Cagliostro a wonderful debut of Miyazaki pre-Ghibli, which all I'll say was very detailed, culturally rich (felt like San Marino) and very engaging. Which brings me to... about 5 that were really really my favourites. Hard to choose just one.

Let's start from Castle in the Sky. Phenomenal oldschool soundtrack like in Nausicaa. Absolutely phenomenal animation which stuns with attention to detail and perfect craftsmanship. It might just be really the most detailed animation from Ghibli that I've seen actually. Very strong concepts converging between militarism, industry and nature. Beautiful conceptual, close to steampunk-type universe, witty and strikes a perfect balance between being a movie for all across kids and adults alike. Pom Poko, another surprise, which mixes, he-he, everything. It's so densely packed with content that I'm not sure even where to begin. It takes the audience through time through the perspective of animals. There's a lot in terms of treatment of environment by humans and its severely negative impact of construction-work, crossed with Japanese religion and beliefs, cross-political considerations of government forms and how they relate to its society. Society-growth from varying perspectives, superstition. Somehow, somewhat, they managed to pack everything into this very neat package and more, which is content-wise beautiful to enjoy.

And I left two more animations, which I believe are primarily taken care of Isao Takahata - so not the Miyazaki, who's most famous from Ghibli. Takahata did also Grave of the Fireflies and Pom Poko and also Only Yesterday which I enjoyed much less. But these titles should already give a bit of a direction on what to expect, as these movies do diverge a bit from the general Ghibli canon. My Neighbors the Yamadas. In theory, it's not much of a great content to be found, as it's almost only but just merely a slice of life story. But what makes it truly exceptional is, two things. Its animation style which is all cartoony in a more caricature-like style and watercolor/crayony overall. I have a weakspot for animation that is realised differently, so there's that, it's a visual feast. The other part is just how freakin' razor-sharp relateable this slice of life is. It's just purely amazing how relateable it is, considering it seems predominantly based on the Japanese family relations but most interactions I could easily connect with despite having been brought up in Europe. Lots of it communicated subliminally, it's all again in sorts packed just like Pom Poko.

I'm not sure if I would give this one my absolute Ghibli favourite but it certainly deserves a big fucking praise. I mean, hey! Would you like to watch a painted animation feature film? I mean sure, most of Ghibli movies already feature painted environments, duh. But what about painted in a seriously traditional-style, as if you were watching an actual traditional-Japanese paintings in motion, the sorts that were painted in watercolor (?) on bamboo sheets with slight pencil exaggeration? The Tale of Princess Kaguya is just exceptional visually, a massive feast for eyes and just receives a massive individual praise from my side for going bold this direction. It doesn't come short just with the visuals, though, as the story is compelling on its own, being stretched across time and dealing with the traditional feudal Japenese relations and present mechanisms of the time.

All in all, what I really enjoyed while going through Ghibli movies, was that the creators often aimed at achieving something more than just an animation. In the end, it's merely a creative device, form of an expression - as I believe. And they were really good at pursuing something more, stretching out the limits and/or becoming the leaders in what could be squeezed out from those limits and providing a very diverse artistic setting.

Avatar: The Last Airbender - When I was a kid (like a kid kid), I watched maniacally cartoons, whether Cartoon Network or Disney Channel. I mean, it was the golden age, right? One network I would always skip, was nickelodeon and that held up over the years, as I really felt it had nothing to offer (even from a kid's point of view) and that kind of translated into me not willing to give this Avatar ago, despite I picked it up here and there. That's okay, wanna go heavier? Well, Full Metal Alchemist: Brotherhood feels like a not-so-distant cousin. And that's a big compliment, since it still tops at my "best anime" list and art-wise (and not just), these two have crazy a lot in common.

Legend of Korra - Oh how happy was I when I discovered that there is a sequel to Avatar. But as kids grow, so does the sequel, with a new, teen Character. Which drives off a bit plot-wise towards more teeny melodrama but not altogether. First season seemed to have got a massive money bump, since the painted quasi-steampunk environments made my mouth water and the animation was of truly exceptional quality. Seasons 2 and 3 dropped ball appropriately but all across there were great educational elements relatively sustained from Avatar and making the show overall a very enjoyable watch. It involves political concepts in a bit broader and detailed manner without necessarily imposing a set view, which I welcomed with open arms. If someone was on the lookout for something anime-like but really western-based, well, here's Nickelodeon producing out this gem.

Undone - I follow Tomek Baginski and literally whatever this guy recommends is always worth a peek. This kinda extends onto the polish gamedev dinosaurs anyways. Great writing and acting, nice artstyle especially when it blends different effects. Bit cringy which would hold me aback at times and it's a slice of life thing but it's fairly short and the performances along with artstyle hold up.

Bit of cyberpunk now. Gunnm, aka, Alita: The Battleangel but in its original anime form. You know, I took a look at the hollywood movie and I was like, um, kinda meh. The anime, however, what I like to see in this genre and at all when I consume media, is having a bit of a stronger emotional touch. Which can lift up even a rather standard, even if style-wise interesting anime. Serial Experiment Lain, wholy sheet what a weird anime. First of all, its structure is off (a bit) (maybe more a bit). Secondly, it very strongly resides in philosophical considerations, of, well, lots circulating around the meaning of life and what it means once its starting to move to the virtual sphere. The visual style is often unsettling and ominous and it doesn't really lead to anything particularly surprising but... you know, some folks tend to say this is a predecessor of Matrix and hey, I think they're right.

Ghost in the Shell: Stand Alone Complex - ha, found on Japanese Netflix with English subtitles which is a surprise. The bigger surprise laid elsewhere, though. The original GITS I guess built its legend based on its highly philosophical nature and and rather sleek yet raw appeal. It was a bit too short, though, yet still a great watch. I figured the rest of the franchise would not stand up to its legend and... I think the first season of Stand Alone Complex did. The problem which remained throughout was its TV-Show-like feel, quite often standardised, there was a strong need to explain stuff by its end etc. But there usually was at least one particularly interesting concept in each episode, with depicted cases becoming stretched out skillfully in terms of what the creators wanted to convey. Action-scenes often felt tasty but even tastier were the ideas dissolved and stirred up to become what it became - something more than just a regular watch, biting at your feelings when needed, making you laugh and sad for the complex realities the depicted characters fell into. Plus, a huge ass cherry on top - the truly exceptional soundtrack which elevated it all and pushed it even further. I'm not saying this show is absolutely magnificent but it exceeded my expectations, even if 2nd season (2nd gig) drops ball on the tastier philosophical cyberpunk approach and instead settles for a typical action-based intrigues, which the show leaned towards in its 1st season but happily extended beyond.

Movies and Books

Limes Inferior - I never really was a book person, which makes me feel like an ass every now and then. I mean, to get hooked on it, it feels like hard work at times and even audiobooks do not necessarily help as you kind of need to concentrate to persevere through normally over 200-pages long titles. That's a fuckload that translates into whoopin' looong hours of listening material!

I've gone through the dystopian 4 of Orwell, Huxley, Bradbury and Zamyatin and.. now that I think about it, which ass did I pull this "dystopian 4" from? So I did a quick google search. And hey! Turns out I know more of it than I previously have thought! Even if in adapted formats. But in all of the quick search results, Limes Inferior was nowhere to be found. Well, I shouldn't be surprised.

..but knowing the other books' backgrounds and the aforementioned equivalents, Limes Inferior as a dystopia feels the closest to the contemporary reality and the author serves it in a surprisingly accessible and deftly written fashion. It's really striking just how much alike this nearly 40-years old world feels. I was looking for something like this, it really does beat its competition and I would love to see an adaptation as its formula feels like a perfect match for a blockbuster tv show.

Limelight and Citizen Kane. I might have to take a closer look at older movies. Limelight was a Charlie Chaplin movie but, you know, I guess the guy was quite famous for... comedy, right? Well, this movie doesn't seem much like so. More of an... hm. Heartfelt ode to the time of greatness? A bit of a story of redemption? Bit of a journey from being a failure to gaining success? Or more like... a window into the concept of life, with one showing that it's definitely worth living through and making the best out of it? It's got a few weirder moments but overall, yeah, solid and quite emotional classic that is with much for the author to convey to its audience. And Citizen Kane, an all-time classic, I was wondering what I'd find. And what I found was... a surprising twist, fairly chaotic structure that still kept at its seams and made sense. Galloping plot that slowly unravelled and had mysteries of its kind. The movie felt quite fresh and playful in terms of how it was directed. I can clearly see why it's an all-time classic. One thing is to break rules but another is to do it masterfully, which transcends not only into direction but also into figuring out what the characters were and why they've done what they've done.

7/10
