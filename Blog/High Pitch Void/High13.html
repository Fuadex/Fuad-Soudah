Carole & Tuesday

Well, Sinichiro Watanabe. That's what I originally thought. Then it turned out there is not much of Sinichiro's handiwork here which saddened me. In fact, this is one of those shows that is more directed towards the teenage audience if not the even younger part. Although, it's not at least due to one or a few scenes featured. Netflix is really playing with fire here, if they're really co-producing it. But I mean, Carole & Tuesday is a nice concept overall. It's got a conceptual frame that stems from criticising the music and the entertainment industry and it does it explicably well. The depth is there and that's what I'm happy about, except the driver of the story is kept in a rather romantic and frankly bit infantile way. It's okay, as the target audience is certainly one that is just simply younger. But seeing Sinichiro as an executive of this show, I had more hopes.

The music featured is actually really good and the spread of styles and their execution is impressive. You wouldn't except any less probably from a show that treats this particular subject but, you know, I wouldn't really jump into conclusions at any stage. There is a lot of original songs that are actually performed in a beautiful way, so it's easier to connect with the characters. The way they unravel and connect with other characters in the show in fact lowers the immersion in my perspective and the parts when they perform kinda balance the lows out. Many of the fillers which would seem so as, are actually insightful and provide an additional important dimension to the problematique in focus of the show. Of course, there are the mains and supporting characters but it is clear in my view that there are larger subjects encapsulated in this show and they are being depicted quite thoroughly. I want to stress this, as even though the show is lead through the lens of the characters often, there is just more to it and some parts of the audience might just get tired of the show due to the infantility of, well, the drive of the show. I also like the bulk of references to metropolises like New York, the show feels a lot like New York and goes hand-in-hand with what's depicted too so.

All in all, Carole & Tuesday is a show that is rather directed towards younger audiences, especially those who have aspirations for resolving and evolving their artistic skills. Even some students would appreciate it. There is a certain amount of depth that make it not just enjoyable but also informing watch, it is multidimensional and quite imaginative in many lengths. It is however driven in a bit too simpleminded way, which can deter some from it. There are some shows that capture similar subjects in a notch better fashion but now that I think about it, there not that many and of those that do exist, have this teenagey feel about it. Like Black Mirror's episode with Miley Cyrus, I'm sure there was something else there too. Perfect Blue breaks through these chains but is there anything that exceptional that loses its innocence of form? I don't recall at the time but it's really not a bad show to follow, even if just to kill time with.

8/10

Weathering With You

I am actually surprised that I am giving this movie such a high note as in certain moments it deserved less. Where do I start? I should really stop paying attention to ads cause they really make a jar of jelly out of my brains. I bit the part where it said 'created by people who made Your Name' but some alarms should've rang when they also advertised that it is much better than the referenced movie. I mean, how much better can it get? Disregarding the fact it loses a lot of its magic on the second run, it would be actually hard to beat this one. And it didn't. In fact, I was a bit seduced by the description of the movie, a short on what's it about. The movie however didn't really elaborate on some of this and placed the focus a bit elsewhere, which on its own is not bad, except there was lots of potential to be explored.

What I found so bad about this movie was its infantility. Its focus was entirely on characters who were not really that interesting to begin with in any way. There were some parts embedded into the movie that felt detached from the whole. There was a lot of stuff that just was without much that would contribute the audience. It was a lot of wobbly wobble and not much to support either. There were no clear motifs attached, nothing really standing out except for the main character who could control the weather. But still nothing much stemmed out of even this and so the movie turned out just simply as bland, nothing more than a cheeseburger you'd get at mcdonalds, despite it being advertised as an experience one-of-a-kind that comes once-in-a-lifetime. And all those 'funny' dialogue lines that came through it, I can understand the audience in the cinema willing to have some fun from watching the movie but laughing at trivialisms and cliched jokes just feels low.

What did actually lift the experience up, was the art. There were some, near classic scenes that were just staggeringly beautiful. A feast for your eyes and even though the colouring of the movie kinda lacked behind, there were some scenes that were just purely beautiful. So beautiful, that suddenly I became generous and gave this a 6, instead of something that it would have much more deserved for.

And the Spielberg all over with the thousands of references attached to it. But somehow some things changed with the new season. Remember my point about me suddenly seeing a surprisingly increased amount of sexual-centred conversations in La Casa Del Papel? Well, guess what's happening in this show too. Suddenly we are being exposed to a wiiiide range of plot lines of which entire nature lays either in romantic relations or the nature of the leading casts sexuality. I don't mind having it in show all in all. I just don't see how having those in swath amounts is going to contribute towards building a stronger show, especially that I don't recall anything Spielbergey (which it doesn't need to be) about it. It's just a lot of unnecessary filler, of which as I pointed already out, there is a massive and almost unsettling amount.

About the unsettling things now. I always saw Stranger Things as a show for families really to enjoy together. But with this new season, I must say I would not let my children watch is. Let alone strong sexual references and horrorlike elements, there is just simply a lot of gore showcased in this one. I mean, think - people melting down. I've seen this before actually done in very horrible ways but pushing the plot forward in very touching ways. But here it's like, having gore just for the sake of having gore. And the CGI of it isn't really that impressive either. Again, this doesn't exactly contribute to the story and make it less digestible for the regular viewer. Also in case of writing, having an intrigue with Russians feels far-fetched. There is a set of fantastic elements attached to this show, that just makes it less believable and that's overall sad to see. One thing is having paranormal abilities demonstrated by leading characters but another to have stuff that just is rather, eh, doesn't match the world depicted in it. That extends onto some of the screenplay too.

Disregarding the downsides, the show remains with a Spielbergey vibe and it's actually nice to follow. Although it's a bit split into different sections which loses on its coherence, it's just almost always harder and less immersive to follow a set of characters being lead in different groups. But it's nice overall. Just the fillers and clashy parts make it a notch worse of an experience than it should.

6/10

And I've never seen The Simpsons (well, a few episodes maybe I did), Family Guy and all the cohort that normal people watch. Altogether with, let's say, How I Met Your Mother series and all the cohort, rather focusing on dramas all in all, where nobody laughs in the background etc. I would like to think it didn't turn me too much into a grumpy fellow (perhaps it did?) but there's a lot of cool features revolving about these series and I do love Animation, a love that I've been reliving from my childhood years when I was watching cartoons like - Samurai Jack, Johnny Bravo etc. Except, with times changing, Samurai Jack apparently came back but specifically as an adult-oriented cartoon shown on Adult Swim. I guess times are really changing then? Even Anime is now probably more seen as a normative genre?

I enjoyed the first season of Disenchantment. It was lightweight, kind of funny here and there. Had a nice course of dynamics and storyline progression, that as if did not progress but with time it did and retained coherence. There was a lot of cool multitude of episodes where as I'd call, the tail was wagging the dog, back and forth. Just the way the show's creators decided to play with the meaning of words and contexts is generally enjoyable. But with this season, it kinda really lost even the mild enjoyability the first season had. I kind of feel as if the creators were not really even that much into actually creating this, there is a nice background and some nice episodes that portray even more interesting themes and universes but the show doesn't pull the tongue out of what the show produces and showcases, which ultimately just severes what more interesting plotlines there may be. Which, to be fair, is kind of a standard for this type of shows but the thing is, many of the singular more standalone episodes lack the progression, the wit and conclusion. Which eventually puts at harms way the overall quality of the show.

Don't get me wrong, it's still an enjoyable thing to watch but you'll be left by the end of it with the feeling that more could've been concentrated into this, so that we, the audience, could end up with a general feel of fulfillment. But ultimately it didn't get even halfway there.
