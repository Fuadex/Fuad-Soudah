This journey was being researched, I think, the most heavily of all my former journeys. First of all, due to its very scarce time-schedule. So, immediately, the prices jump like crazy! Twice or thrice as much as usual. To add into this, it is a good season to really go on holidays in this region, as in theory it's winter. Thing is, winter as winter, they don't really get snow but it's just a dry season. Usually all they get is: Dry and hot, Humid and hot, Humid and superhot seasons. Welcome to South East Asia. I was going in the dry and hot, although, honestly, looking at weather and knowing my own experiences from different seasons around the world, it looked quite civil overall. Anyone looking from elsewhere would think it's a pure aberration and a complete jockey and lunatic would do this, not knowing how to spend money in life. Truth is, that was partially the case. At first I established some routes. I planned to keep it a bit more civil and do perhaps half of South East Asia, due to deemed costs. I also considered Japan, since there were some interesting offers and, truth be told, I love Japan, so much I want to comeback and see it properly this time. But the more reason, to just head up there and observe a bit of the environment and life, being in this microcosm, perhaps under the wraps of snow falling down. But, I'll need to keep this away for a bit, there should be time in the future. Instead, I opted for a bit newer direction, yet very unknown in so many lengths.

In parallel to looking for flight connections and prices, I'd look at visa conditions. The good thing, is that in pretty much all of the region, you can quite easily roll into. Some are visa free, some are visa on arrival. The only exception was really Vietnam, which required an application prior, although they didn't call it a visa. More of a letter of invitation. It's sort of what USA uses as ESTA, where you need to file in an application and it's cheap and with this, you can kind of get the visa. Except, for USA, you don't really need a visa afterward with ESTA, with Vietnam you need to state the type you'll need. It's not that complicated. But the process is fairly fast, also processing time. I think the American system is fairy-tale-like fast, though. As once I had an urgent situation, which I managed to resolve within one hour, during which an application was filed in and processed within minutes with an approved result. Especially impressive, since I was doing this for another person. In any case, other countries were very much straightforward. But some of the Visa on Arrival countries, obviously, required fees to be regulated. And that was a bit of a sad reality seen through my eyes.

The first combinations covered most of SEA excluding Myanmar, Laos and Brunei. Sometimes Hong Kong could not get counted too. The problem was, that certain directions were just too expensive. Flights to or especially from Brunei were insane, the standard cost would be about 350 AU$ one-way and this was something I could not agree to. My limits usually stuck to a 100 AU$ per flight and I didn't intend to bend it much. In the end, I'd discover some hacks. For instance, during Christmas season, Brunei to Philippines would be excessively expensive, but the other way, not at all. Some fligts out to Jakarta would be cheap, though. Flights to Kuala Lumpur or Singapore would almost always have a cheap connection. Flights within countries would usually be cheap, which, after some time, I added 2 to 2. One thing you'll need to understand is, that the countries around this region, at times, have really massive populations. Indonesia is a quarter billion. Philippines is over a hundred mil. Vietnam is almost a hundred mil. Other countries in the region are perhaps not as populous but still relatively quite. This would explain a fair bit after having visited but even before, how come the domestic flights are relatively cheap. International flights would also usually come with taxes which would compose at times even a huge chunk of the ticket's price. So even if I wanted to go lower, it would be close to impossible getting a better deal even at a better season. Also, keep in mind, that with such surmountable populations, much of the region was colonized. And many of the countries had different colonists: French, English, Spanish, Portugese, Dutch. Later on, Japanese, American and many other influences. Honestly, it's a very intelectually rich region to learn about and fascinating, after having seen it all, to experience first-hand. The differences, being, legacy, everything that confluenced in the matter of past centuries and deriving from times that reach much further prior. It also opens a completely new perspective on things.

Ridiculous. Some tickets could be booked via Google, which made things extremely smooth. But sometimes it would become unnecessarily weird, later on, seeing how my name printed on my boarding pass coming from Singapore to Sydney would be just Fuad Soudah, instead of Fuad Philip Soudah. Cheap in this region. So cheap, you can essentially rent a car with a driver for 20 US$. I swear, if this was a joke, I'd come with a more hedonic tone on this one. For 8 hours, in a car, in Siem Reap, I spent 20 US$ and gave 3 dollars tip to the guy who got me riding around Angkor Wat. This is the reality in this region. Not everywhere but it's essentially unbelieveably cheap to the point, that you can afford daily drivers in the price of your accommodation, essentially. In Vietnam, a Bahm Mi sandwich would cost an equivalent of 1 dollar. And it would be delicious. At the airport in Vietnam, the same one would cost 7-8 US$ and not be nearly as good (probably, didn't try). Endgame, I thought I wouldn't have to care about expenditures on food in this region. But in the end, with so little time on my hands, it starts to become slightly an issue, that is, to pack in food in the city and sightseeing, so ultimately, I would be forced to feast a bit at airports. Kind of a bit of a sad reality but not necessarily, as at times, I'd find some really decent places to eat at airports. And even then, I'd deduct that the prices would not exceed what I'd experience back in Australia, so I felt it was all good in the end. Can you imagine, getting a decent meal at an airport for an equivalent of 10 US$, with everything? Again, welcome to South East Asia. And they'd even wear Santa hats around.
