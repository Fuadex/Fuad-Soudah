Research Questions:

- How open are digital companies in terms of sharing information on their users via API (application programming interface)?
-- (Spin-off question) How much data and of what kind may be collated from which digital services and in what forms is it being shared?

- How does a collated historical summary of information about a person affect that person and how can the person practically benefit from it?
-- (Spin-off questions) What kind of information do we want to learn about ourselves? Is there any information that we would prefer not to know about ourselves? How do we feel about having all sorts of data about us being collected and how would we feel about it being collected in which instances?

- What are the existing hardware-based solutions in terms of collecting user-data, how is it being currently utilised and how does the user benefit from it?
- (Spin-off questions) How do the companies benefit from the user-data? Are there any restrictions established by the companies or governments in terms of collecting user-data? What array of software is being used in which geographical areas and how does that affect the openness of data shared by its users?


Keywords: wearables, api, self-tracking

Significance: An array of data is being collected on us by a range of companies, all supposedly for the benefit of us. However, there is no tool that I know of which would collate all this data and present how we evolved as a person, so that we can see changes that have happened and the direction taken. Despite that we share data all the time, we might not be getting the full potential/benefit from it. I'm curious to see, how far can this be stretched out and how may this affect the users themselves, along with potential outcomes for a variety of other stakeholders...

..For instance private or government-owned companies, which may decide to increase the openness of their platforms for users to be more willing to share their data, hence increasing the transparency of those companies (one scenario, I guess it's a good idea to come up with those?)

Expected Outcomes: A fully working cross-platform application making use of a range of modular APIs that can work in convergence and synergetically towards providing information potentially surprising, benefiting and unknown originally to its users. The users' approach towards the idea of tracking self will be researched beforehand as well as how the application impacts themselves and the transformation of how they thought and felt before they had access to the application and after they had used it.

Methodology:

The general approach would be to establish good guy Donald Norman's Double Diamond, a frameset to take upon any challenge in the design world but considering it is research and the design-world is ever-changing, I expect as much to shift (iteratively) in these regards.

Background research covering: research papers, publications, journals, magazines, surveys conducted, existing hardware (devices and modules) and how it changed throughout the years (therefore, including trends), existing digital platforms in Australia and abroad, and the openness of their API (and how it works). Considering the topic of tracking may be also controversial, it might be a good idea to feature potential misuses and worries backed by influential literature and other media and how it was envisioned, from Orwell to Cyberpunk, Minority Report etc.
