Each day I spring out for breakfast. Jet lag only with time flicked the switch on and generally I wake up at 6, half an hour before a random number of Sodom bracing up to transform my room into an abattoir (what I meant by this, was that I had Sodom - Get What Your Deserve as an alarm set, I used to listen to a lot of thrash metal and I think they released a fairly good album at the time) that made me get up from bed to throw my phone the hell away from my balkony. To make things pleasant, I'd wake up about half an hour before the alarm. Bread is typically cheap and I like to grab myself a sandwich with a cucumber... tomato and ham or maybe fromage cheese (I guess any French person will start putting up some question marks up in the air, what I meant by this was like this type of cheese that you can spread out on bread, really nice and fresh-tasting). Such deal is 2 Euros, yet this sandwich + a bottle of cola + caffeine or Spezi which is coke + some form of lemonade and caffeine and I am ready for another day, another battle. And that, despite every single next day felt somehow a little bit less interesting... Perhaps I should become a traveller. That said, in fact somehow I am one already...

At this stage, looking back from 2020 I should specifically point out that these early years felt recursively quite dark to me. Interestingly, it is said that Senhsucht II and III were written on the same day, along with IV, V and VI. You can clearly devise from the tone I was writing, that suddenly it all started becoming a bit more eerie, I was mixing some more darker and regular tones of just me carrying out with life. I'll get back to overviewing this but it's good to remark that, in the course of my 11 months, before and after I had lots of stuff in my head that kind of kept me back and aback, even if I managed to complete quite a journey overall, it kind of came ultimately with a mental pricetag attached that went up and down over my lifespan.

Critical Dillema

I'm not entirely sure what I was doing on Wednesday. I visited a bit Olydorf, took some photos (wasn't that on Thursday?). I any case, on Thursday apart from the regular course, I went out into my own neighbourhood and went off to the local government building, whatever it's actually called. Figure this, I boing in through a building and over there a mr guard in a broken English expressed that I can't go in, as it's full. In the meantime, the place was about to close in... an hour and 10 minutes. Well, how can this be like this and whatever, I ask in this case if there's another bureau. There is. Really freakin' far but perhaps I can make it in half an hour before closure...

Hehs. I made it. Nobody asked me to leave. The lady in the window routed me left and right and left and something something. 5 minutes I was looking for this something something, which turned out to be just a (printed) number. And with this number I was waiting for 1.5h, or maybe an hour... I got in, good mate bureaucrat had a really great command of English, like super great mayonnaise. I provided my documents, passports, put down my signatures. 5-10 minutes, 'Welcome to Munich', I receive a document and thanks, bye bye. Now I can set up my bank account.

Oh, I forgot, on Wednesday I went to the shop. Well what? I know you don't give af but I forgot to ask for a plastic bag and I had to spring home with toilet paper packed into my blouse's pocket. I was in pain! Especially, that the shop I had was much closer, 5 minutes instead of 45 minutes away. Here I should add that the Aldi I took a hike to the other day was, in fact, fairly far away, as I had to tread kind of around and across the entire olympic village + park. It's a beautiful walk nonetheless but it does take 45 minutes to get there. I wasn't aware there was a local shop just 5 minutes away from where I lived, though. Kinda a fun fact y'all. At times I find places much further than what's just pretty much right at my grasp.

Let's roll forward to Thursday. I set up my account in 10-15 minutes and I was in summary a bit sad, as the process looked weird. The mate did not tell me what sort of account he was setting up, he just asked: will I use the internet? Will I be doing it? He explained a few things quite superficially etc. However, such experience felt 50:50, in any case I setup the account, I had to only fulfill a few formalities: I asetup some sort of a key to my internet banking, then I converted my account to have had an account statement electronically (instead of needing to have to fly each month to print it out WTF) and my card should arrive at my place next week. Nice, eh? Looking back, there were a few weird things, like this statement printing thing, I'd see people doing this every now and then. I'd get a weird 0 Euro bill each month I think that was for this. Also, the account was setup as a jugendgirokonto which meant it was for youth. This was perfect, as I beared literally no expenses in such case until I turn I think 25. It wasn't a visa/mastercard card but instead an EC karte, so it could work only with German ATMs rather and I could withdraw up to 200 Euros. Still some extra internet banking was allowed and I used it.

By the end of the day there was supposed to be an Olydorf sightseeing + I wanted to buy myself a router, two things kind of overlapped. Unfortunately, throughout the vacation period (I was at Olydorf BEFORE the semester started, with a language course kinda spanning through), router can be bought only between 8-9PM on Thursdays (some places in Germany have weird opening hours, like once a day for just an hour like that place). During that time you can always fix issues regarding the internet etc. I decided to still take part in the TourDeOlydorf and yet I managed to buy this router during this tour as well. I missed seeing OlyDisco and some other point, yet I received a free beer in Bierstube (local pub, I'll explain in depth in some blog about what exactly was at Olydorf and why it's the best place on earth for students to live in). In Bierstube, I broke the ice, had a conversation with people and it turned out that I was doing quite well, as some folks from the student staff remained and the rest just departed (there wasn't a lot of us) after which they invited me for sheesha. I think that's the first time I met a group of very welcoming folks that I'd meet every now and then at Olydorf. I think this was still my first week, day 3 in fact.

Sheesha was really cool. With 3 pipes. With a taste of... what taste was this. Peach-mint? They smoked something with the coco coal (yeah, could bear a different name in English), or something, which they'd heat up for a long time. They offered white wine. It was really cool for a third day in Germany, I even managed to get to know somebody, have a drink and it turned out that one of the folks lives just a few doors down my bungalow row. Everything according to plan.

They were building this village during the cold war, so perhaps that's the reason?

And despite this, I managed by far to sort out lots of stuff, although it's still relatively not enough. In which, I had many things left for later, due to which lots continuously accumulate into a pack that continuously fade away in my eyes, not disappearing altogether, yet remaining on the horizon. From stone cold tropics right into a scorching hot moderate climate. Despite that these are practically the same climate zones.

It's weird, oh weird.

I think what I meant by this was that I was leaving a country that is considered very hot, in fact even extremely hot, yet, in Sydney during winter, temepratures can drop even down to 0, with an average of 17 as I know. That may still not be considered cold by relatively high humidity can amplify the feeling of cold in many circumstances. There were also some other feelings in development, from the perspective of things, I wish they didn't happen, otherwise my exchange might've become a much more pleasant experience. Instead, I had a lot of time and the structure of the course allowed for... well, myself, with whom I did not have clear idea of what I was looking forward to do.
